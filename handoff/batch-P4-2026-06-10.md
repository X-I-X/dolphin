## Content notes

### What these pages are
The Elvar series documents one of the earliest attempts at cross-species phoneme reproduction. Scott McVay (at Coconut Grove, likely early 1960s) taught Elvar the dolphin lists of nonsense syllables — consonant/vowel combinations in sequences up to ten. Elvar would reproduce them. The famous anecdote: when a Bell Labs researcher dropped his list mid-session and said "correction," Elvar only reproduced the syllables given *after* the correction — demonstrating that Elvar had tracked the correction protocol, not just the sounds.

The four recorded vocalizations:
- **"ca-ra"** — two-syllable sequence
- **"oh-li-vey"** — three-syllable sequence
- **"wah-ooh"** — two-syllable sequence
- **"en-ain-eet"** — three-syllable sequence

### elvar33x.html — classroom context
The page title (from the original) is `Wait until the noise subsides, and then begin class.` — this was the framing instruction for the classroom listening exercise. The page presents all four recordings as an audio lesson. The `elvar01.gif` image (Scott and Elvar photo, archived to Cloudinary) is shown in a slider with `group-00.jpg` AI placeholder.

### DCR audio recovery potential
Unlike the Peter dolphin DCR files (which were Howe's personal recordings), these Elvar recordings are from a well-known experiment cited in Lilly's books. There is a possibility these .dcr files exist in archive.org or academic collections. If recovered, they could be converted to MP3/OGG — significant preservation win.
